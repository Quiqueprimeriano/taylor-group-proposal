Paper 01

Reconfiguring Work: Change Management in the Age of Gen AI

Author: Erik Roth, McKinsey & Company — August 2025

“Piloting gen AI is easy, but creating value is hard.”

Core thesis

This paper presents a five-step change management framework for implementing generative AI at organizational scale. The central argument is that technology adoption without organizational transformation delivers negligible returns.

The Five-Step Framework

1 — Craft a North Star Based on Outcomes, Not Tools

  • Define an aspirational vision of human-AI collaboration, not software adoption
  • Must be simple enough to understand universally, bold enough to inspire
  • Concept of “Minimum Viable Organizations” (MVOs): agent swarms that oversee most work with minimal human oversight for back-office; higher-touch functions remain human-powered
  • The North Star should accommodate technological evolution and address impact on the talent lifecycle

2 — Build Trust with Data, Governance & Enterprise Wisdom

  • Gen AI high performers (companies attributing 10%+ EBITDA to gen AI) invest 2× more in trust-enabling activities
  • Companies investing in AI trust achieve 2× higher revenue growth (10%+)
  • Human-in-the-loop checkpoints to detect hallucinations, biases, and data leakage
  • Ground AI systems in organizational context: proprietary research, customer logs, institutional knowledge
Case Study

Morgan Stanley trained gen AI on 100,000+ research reports with rigorous evaluation frameworks → 98% adoption by wealth management teams.

3 — Reimagine Workflows: Three Phases of Evolution

PhaseDescriptionHuman Role
Phase 1Stand-alone agents — discrete tasks within workflowsAI as tool
Phase 2Agent groups — multiple agents complete full end-to-end workflowsHuman manages & oversees
Phase 3Agentic swarms — fully autonomous MVOsHuman oversight only

4 — Rethink Organizational Structures

  • MVOs work best for repetitive, logic-based tasks (e.g., invoice processing)
  • Augmented teams remain essential for customer-facing roles requiring human touch
  • New roles emerging: AI workflow optimizer, automation product owner
  • Employees in automated roles need reskilling paths, not displacement

5 — Empower Employees as Change Agents

  • Typical transformation involves only 2% of employees; best performers involve 7% (2× success rate); top performers 21–30% (3× success rate)
  • “Middle-out” strategy: CEOs role-model from the top, millennial champions (62% report high AI expertise vs. 22% of boomers) evangelize from the middle
  • Federated agent creation: McKinsey enabled all employees to build agents → 150 centralized + 17,000 employee-created agents, zero tech debt

Key Takeaway

Formal training is the single greatest multiplier for AI adoption. Without it, usage plateaus at ~50%. With structured programs that include role-modeling, onboarding integration, and superuser communities, adoption reaches 87%+. The tool must be treated as a team member, not a tool.

Paper 02

The Change Agent: Goals, Decisions & Implications for CEOs in the Agentic Age

Authors: Alex Singla, Alexander Sukharevsky, Lari Hämäläinen et al. — McKinsey Quarterly, October 2025

“The puck is moving faster than it used to. Leaders need to skate to where the puck is going, not where it’s been.”

Core thesis

This paper addresses the strategic and operational implications of agentic AI for organizational leadership. It argues that incremental adoption will fail — what’s needed is a redesign of operating models, talent strategies, and governance structures.

The Agent Spectrum

AI adoption is not binary. The paper defines four levels of organizational maturity:

LevelWhat It IsImpactExample
Agentic Labor Agents as individual productivity tools +20–30% personal throughput AI drafts, researches, codes for one person
Task & Workflow Automation Automating existing processes end-to-end 20–40% faster cycles; 60–80% cost reduction in contact centers Automated call routing and resolution
Functional Agentic Workflows Entire domain workflows redesigned around agent teams Efficiency + revenue uplift + satisfaction Financial planning reimagined with AI reordering tasks
Cross-Functional Systems Autonomous agents working across functions with high-level decisioning 70–80% cost reduction per transaction 24/7 field service dispatch; mortgage approval in seconds
Case Study

Universal bank modernization: 3 engineers + 100 agents (supervised by 5 humans) completed an entire modernization lifecycle → 50%+ reduction in time and labor costs.

The 2–3 Year CEO Roadmap

Years 1–2: Setting the Course & Creating Momentum

  • Focus on operating cost reduction (10–20% in targeted functions)
  • 25–50% of employees working with agents; “fluency” growing measurably
  • First “lighthouse” project reimagines a complete end-to-end process (e.g., order-to-cash targeting >70% automation)
  • Demand for certain roles begins to decrease (coding agents reduce burden 50–100%)
  • New role demand: AI workflow optimizer, automation product owner

Years 2–3: Scaling Across the Organization

  • First lighthouse hits >90% automation with effective escalation protocols
  • 75%+ adoption across all functions; each professional manages ≥1 agent, many 3–5
  • Agent-to-agent interactions crossing functional boundaries become the norm
  • FTE reduction: 30–40% in software development, 75% workload reduction in financial planning/reporting
  • Shift from activity metrics to P&L impact measurement

Four Critical Mindsets for CEOs

1. Reimagine What’s Possible

Value comes from rearchitected workflows, not automated tasks. Think agent-first organizations.

2. Act with Urgency

Wait-and-see is the riskiest strategy. Early learnings compound into irreversible competitive advantage.

3. Tackle Scale Now

Build vs. buy vs. partner decisions, trust frameworks, and talent capabilities take longer than expected.

4. Everyone Becomes an Agent Leader

Skills: building agents, training them, assigning tasks, correcting work, chaining them for complex workflows.

The “Agent Factory” Concept

A central organizational hub responsible for:

  • Identifying workflows for agent-led redesign
  • Managing cross-functional teams (AI + IT + domain experts)
  • Scaling successful redesigns across the organization
  • Governance to prevent uncontrolled agent sprawl
  • Cost/performance tracking, access control, security, and privacy
Amazon Parallel

In 2002, Jeff Bezos mandated that all teams expose their services as APIs — not as a technology preference, but as an organizational mandate. The same top-down mandate is now required for agentic AI: every function must be “agentifiable.”

Key Takeaway

The greatest value doesn’t come from augmenting individuals — it comes from rearchitecting entire workflows and organizations around agent-first systems. This requires CEO-level involvement, operating model redesign, new talent strategies, and updated performance management frameworks. Incremental adoption won’t work.

Synthesis

Where the Two Papers Converge

Paper 1 provides the “how” (five steps of change management). Paper 2 provides the “what and where” (agent spectrum + CEO roadmap). Together, they form a complete picture:

ThemePaper 1 (Change Mgmt)Paper 2 (Agentic Age)
Adoption Formal training: 50% → 87% 75%+ across all functions in 2–3 years
Structure Champions (7–30% of employees) Agent Factory as central hub
Evolution 3 phases (individual → groups → swarms) 4 levels of the Agent Spectrum
Leadership CEO role-models, millennials evangelize Everyone must become an “Agent Leader”
Urgency Only 2% of employees typically involved Wait-and-see is the riskiest strategy
Governance Human-in-the-loop, oversight committee Organizational mandate (Amazon API model)

The bottleneck is not technology — it’s organizational readiness. Companies that invest in trust, training, and structural transformation will capture exponential returns. Those that treat AI as a tool upgrade will fall behind.

Five Questions Every CEO Should Be Asking

  1. How will agentic AI challenge our sources of differentiation — and create new ones?
  2. How could agents disrupt our core business and relationships with customers, suppliers, and partners?
  3. How do we prepare for and manage a hybrid human-agent workforce while preserving values and culture?
  4. What’s our talent strategy — in-house vs. outsourced, open-source vs. single-platform?
  5. What should our investment roadmap look like to meet near-term goals and lay transformation foundations?
Application

How This Adds Value to the Taylor Group Proposal

Taylor Group ($85M revenue, 321 employees, 95-year history) is a textbook case for the frameworks described above. Here are six specific ways these McKinsey insights can strengthen the TransformAZ engagement:

1. Quantify the Champions Program

The current proposal mentions a “champions program” in the People & Change pillar, but without hard numbers. McKinsey data provides the evidence:

Recommendation

Target 22 champions minimum (7% of 321 employees) to double success probability. Ideal target: 70–95 champions (21–30%) to triple it. Champions should not just be ambassadors — they should be active agent creators, following McKinsey’s federated model (17,000 employee-created agents).

2. Reposition the Retainer as “Agent Factory”

Taylor has no CTO. No one is filling the strategic technology governance role. The STABILIZATION retainer should be repositioned from “ongoing advisory” to “Fractional AI Operations Lead” — the Agent Factory function that McKinsey identifies as structurally necessary for organizations scaling agentic AI.

“Advisory retainer” “Fractional AI Operations Lead” Fills a structural gap, not just a service gap
“Ongoing support” “Agent Factory as a Service” McKinsey validates as organizational necessity
Optional add-on Strategic continuation Higher perceived value = higher LTV

3. Use the Agent Spectrum as a Maturity Roadmap

Replace the generic “Market Insights” section with a Taylor-specific maturity roadmap mapped to the Agent Spectrum:

This gives the CEO (Dean Marks) a long-term vision and positions the retainer as the vehicle to get there.

4. Add a Knowledge Base Activation Module

The proposal’s costliest finding is asset management ($500K–$1.2M/year in losses from rebuilding assets that should be reused). The Morgan Stanley case provides a direct parallel:

Opportunity

Taylor has 95 years of project data, designs, and asset records. Training ClickUp Brain or a custom agent on this knowledge base could dramatically reduce asset duplication. Morgan Stanley achieved 98% adoption with a similar approach. This module should be a named deliverable in IMPLEMENTATION.

5. Solve the Generational Gap with the “Middle-Out” Strategy

Taylor’s CFO (“pushing 60, stuck in their ways”) represents the adoption challenge. McKinsey’s data shows 62% of millennial managers report high AI expertise vs. 22% of boomers. The strategy:

  • Top-down: CEO Dean Marks role-models AI adoption (he already initiated the engagement — strong signal)
  • Middle-out: Identify millennial managers across Taylor’s three offices as natural champions
  • Bottom-up: Don’t spend energy convincing the CFO directly — let results from their direct reports do the convincing

6. Anchor Training Impact with Hard Numbers

The People & Culture pillar currently reads as a “soft” deliverable. McKinsey’s adoption data transforms it into a measurable outcome:

87%

Including this data in the proposal converts the training investment from a cost center into a documented multiplier for every other deliverable in the program.

These six enhancements don’t change the proposal’s structure — they strengthen it with McKinsey-validated data, reposition the retainer as a strategic necessity, and give Taylor’s leadership a clear maturity roadmap that extends the engagement’s value horizon from months to years.
