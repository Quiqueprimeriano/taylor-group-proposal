Section 01

Hourly Rate Analysis — £150/hr

The proposal quotes a rate of £150 per hour, applied uniformly across both phases. To assess whether this rate is correctly positioned, we benchmarked it against 2025-2026 UK consulting rate data across multiple segments.

UK Market Rate Benchmarks

Segment Hourly Rate (GBP) Daily Rate (GBP)
Junior / Early-Career Consultant £35 – £55 £250 – £400
Mid-Level / Experienced Independent £55 – £165 £400 – £1,200
Senior Specialist / Boutique £165 – £345 £1,200 – £2,500
Operations & Transformation (SME) £65 – £200 £500 – £1,500
Big 4 — Senior Consultant ~£190 ~£1,500
Big 4 — Senior Manager ~£270 ~£2,100
TransformAZ (current) £150 £1,050 – £1,200

Sources: Business Accounting UK, YunoJuno 2025 Freelancer Rates Report, G-Cloud UK Government Data, Slideworks / GSA Federal Supply Schedule.

What's working

£150/hr sits at the upper end of mid-level and the lower end of senior specialist — a comfortable position for a boutique consultancy. It's neither so low that it signals inexperience, nor so high that it triggers cost objections from an SME client.

Strategic concern

Exposing the hourly rate in the proposal body invites commodity comparison. The client may anchor on "£150/hr" and compare with freelancers at £50–£80/hr found on platforms like YunoJuno or Toptal. The rate is justified — but the framing puts it at risk of challenge.

Recommendation: Move the hourly breakdown to an appendix or the Statement of Work. Present fees as project-based in the main proposal.

Section 02

Pricing Model — Hourly vs. Alternatives

The proposal uses an hourly billing model (hours × rate = fee). While the total is expressed as a fixed amount per phase, the underlying calculation is fully visible. Industry data shows this model is increasingly out of favour.

Industry Pricing Model Adoption

Model Adoption Earning Potential
Hourly Billing 29% of consultants Lowest — caps at available hours
Daily Rate Common in interim market Low-Medium
Fixed / Project-Based Growing — 44% offer productised services Medium — rewards efficiency
Retainer Common for ongoing advisory Medium-High — predictable revenue
Value-Based 17.3% of consultants Highest — 51% achieve £10K+ projects

Sources: Consulting Success Fee Survey 2024, Deltek Consulting Pricing Report, Melisa Liberman Consulting Statistics 2025.

A critical data point: 73% of clients prefer value-based pricing tied to measurable outcomes. Yet only 17% of consultants offer it — and 39% say they've never tried it because they don't know how.

Key issue

The current model shows hours × rate = fee in full detail. This shifts the client's evaluation from "Is solving this problem worth £3,000?" to "Is this person worth £150 per hour?" — a fundamentally harder question to answer yes to.

Recommended Alternative

Present fees as project-based with no hourly breakdown in the main document:

Phase 1 — Discovery & Roadmap: £3,000

Includes all diagnostic work, stakeholder sessions, and documented deliverables over a 2-week period.

The hourly detail can live in the Statement of Work or an appendix — available if requested, but not the anchor for the pricing conversation.

Section 03

Total Investment — £9,000

The proposal prices the full engagement at £9,000 GBP (£3,000 Phase 1 + £6,000 Phase 2) for approximately 60 hours of senior consulting across 6 weeks.

Market Comparison

Engagement Type Typical Range (UK) TransformAZ
Diagnostic & Roadmap £3,000 – £25,000 £3,000 (floor)
Implementation Sprint £10,000 – £75,000 £6,000 (below floor)
Operational Advisory Retainer (monthly) £2,000 – £6,000 Equivalent ~£6K/month
Full diagnostic + implementation (SME) £15,000 – £50,000 £9,000 (well below)

Sources: Matt Haycox Consulting Pricing 2025, Asamby Consulting, Consultancy.uk SME Market Report.

Below market

The total engagement is priced at the floor of the market for discovery alone, and below market for execution. The scope described — process mapping, technology assessment, workflow automation, data integration, stakeholder validation, and a scalability playbook across two areas of transformation — would typically command £15,000–£30,000 from a boutique consultancy in the UK.

The Underpricing Risk

UK SME research shows that 49% of small businesses avoid consultants due to perceived over-inflated costs. However, underpricing creates an equal but less obvious problem: the client unconsciously assigns less value to the work, expects less rigour in delivery, and may not prioritise their own involvement — all of which degrade outcomes.

Possible Justifications

  • Land-and-expand strategy: If the £9,000 engagement is designed to build trust for larger follow-on work, the pricing is defensible as a deliberate investment in the relationship.
  • First formal engagement: For a new consultancy establishing its track record, competitive pricing reduces friction and builds case studies.
  • Client sensitivity: If the specific client has signalled budget constraints, meeting them where they are can be strategically sound.
Recommendation

If pricing must stay at £9,000, consider tiered options (see Section 5) to anchor the base price against a higher alternative. If pricing flexibility exists, a total of £12,000–£15,000 would be more aligned with market expectations and would not price out a typical SME.

Section 04

Engagement Structure — 2-Phase Model

Phase Model Comparison

Model Phases Used by
This proposal 2 (Discovery + Execution) —
Standard consulting 4 (Discovery, Planning, Implement, Report) Most mid-tier firms
Peter Block model 5 phases Academic/training
9Lenses framework 7 phases Enterprise consulting
What's working

A 2-phase model is valid and client-friendly. It reduces decision complexity and the "Phase 2 confirmed after Phase 1" gate is aligned with industry best practice. Selling discovery as a standalone paid engagement (not free) is specifically recommended by consulting advisors.

Discovery-to-Execution Ratio

Industry standard allocates 10–15% of total project budget to discovery. The current proposal allocates 33%.

Metric Industry Standard This Proposal
Discovery % of total budget 10 – 15% 33%
Discovery % of total hours 10 – 15% 33%
Execution hours (2 transformation areas) Variable 40 hours

Sources: 8Allocate, Syndicode, SolveTech, The Visible Authority.

Imbalance

The client may perceive this as "2 weeks of studying, only 4 weeks of doing" — and question whether 40 hours is sufficient to execute a meaningful transformation across two complex operational areas. This is especially relevant because Phase 2 includes implementation, integration, procedures, validation, and a scalability playbook.

Recommendation: Consider presenting Phase 2 with an estimated range (e.g., £6,000–£10,000) rather than a fixed fee, with final scope defined by Phase 1 findings.

Missing Structural Elements

  • Handover / closeout phase: What happens when the 40 hours end? The client will implicitly worry about this.
  • Post-implementation support: Even a brief "2-week check-in period included" would add perceived value at minimal cost.
  • Timeline visual: A simple week-by-week bar showing Phase 1 → Review Gate → Phase 2 would make the engagement more tangible.
Section 05

Proposal Content Review

Strengths

Strong elements

Understanding Your Business is the standout section. It demonstrates genuine comprehension of the franchise model, the distinction between franchisor-mandated and back-office systems, and identifies two specific, defensible transformation areas. This level of specificity is the single strongest differentiator in consulting proposals.

  • Two focused transformation areas — avoids the "boil the ocean" trap that kills execution quality
  • Concrete deliverables per phase — the client knows exactly what they receive
  • Governance section — meeting cadence, reporting, and contact points signal operational maturity
  • Personal signature from Fermín — research shows 60–80% of B2B decisions are made before formal contact; personal trust is critical
  • Phase gate mechanism — "Phase 2 confirmed after Phase 1 review" reduces client risk perception

Gaps — What's Missing

A. No Quantification of the Client's Problem

The proposal describes what is wrong but never puts a number on it. Winning consulting proposals quantify the cost of inaction:

"If manual reconciliation consumes 20 hours/week at £25/hr of internal cost, that represents £26,000/year in direct personnel cost alone — before accounting for errors, reporting delays, and missed early-warning signals."

This type of framing anchors the fee as small relative to the problem. Without it, £9,000 is judged as an absolute number in a vacuum.

B. No ROI or Value Framing

The industry standard is for fees to represent 10–20% of value delivered, with a target client ROI of 5–10x. The proposal's value paragraph is generic:

"Compared to the ongoing operational cost of manual processes, misallocated resources, and delayed financial reporting, this represents a targeted investment in sustainable operational capacity."

This should be specific. If the transformation saves the client £50,000–£90,000 per year (realistic for automation of two manual workflows), then £9,000 represents a 5.5–10x ROI — an exceptional value proposition that should be stated explicitly.

Sources: Consulting Quest, Alan Weiss "Value-Based Fees", Precursive ROI Framework.

C. No Case Studies or Proof Points

Winning proposals include inline mini case studies. Even if TransformAZ is a new entity, Fermín's prior experience can be referenced:

"In a similar engagement with a multi-brand retail group, we reduced reconciliation cycle time by 65% within the first 8 weeks."

Proposals with customised proof points consistently outperform generic submissions.

D. KPIs Are Directional, Not Targeted

The current KPIs describe what will be measured but not what success looks like. Adding estimated targets — even with a "to be validated in Phase 1" caveat — signals ambition and confidence:

"Response Time — Target: reduce from ~48hr average to <12hr (to be validated during discovery)"

E. No Pricing Tiers

Best practice is to present 3 pricing options (basic, standard, premium). Research shows:

  • 73% of clients choose the middle option
  • Tiering increases average deal size by 20–30%
  • It gives the client a sense of control and reduces the "yes/no" decision to a "which one" decision

F. Payment Terms Absent

No mention of when payment is due. Standard structures include: 100% upfront per phase, or 50% at kickoff + 50% at delivery. This absence can cause awkward post-signature conversations and delays.

Section 06

Risk & Watchpoints

High risk — Scope creep in Phase 2

40 hours to implement automation, data integration, operating procedures, stakeholder validation, and a scalability playbook across two transformation areas is extremely tight. If reality is more complex than anticipated (and it usually is), the risk is either delivering partially or working unpaid hours — both of which damage the engagement.

Mitigation: Add an explicit scope adjustment clause. If Phase 1 reveals that Phase 2 scope exceeds what 40 hours can deliver, the scope and fee are adjusted by mutual agreement before execution begins.

Medium risk — Client dependency

The "Client Involvement" section mentions timely provision of system access and documentation, but does not define what "timely" means. If the client takes 2 weeks to provide system access, does the timeline extend? Does the fee adjust?

Mitigation: Add a clause specifying that delays in client-side provisioning will extend timelines proportionally, and that material delays (e.g., >5 business days) may require scope reassessment.

Medium risk — No exit or pivot clause

If, 10 hours into Phase 1, it becomes clear that the transformation areas are different from what was anticipated, there is no explicit mechanism to pivot. The proposal's scope is locked to "Compliance & Repairs" and "Income Reconciliation" — what happens if the real opportunity is elsewhere?

Mitigation: Add language noting that Phase 1 may refine or replace the initial transformation areas based on diagnostic findings, with client approval.

Competitive risk — No differentiation barrier

The proposal could be replicated by any competent consultant. There is no proprietary methodology, framework, or tool that creates a switching cost. Consider naming the methodology (e.g., "The TransformAZ Operating Model") or referencing proprietary diagnostic tools.

Priority Actions (Ranked by Impact)

  1. Remove hourly rate from the proposal body — present as project fee; hourly detail in SOW only
